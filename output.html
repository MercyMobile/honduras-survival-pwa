Fer-de-lance (Bothrops asper)

Also known as: Tommygoff, Lancehead | Barba Amarilla, Terciopelo

A hemotoxic viper growing 1-2 meters. Brown/tan with X-shaped markings and triangular head. Most dangerous snake in Honduras - causes most snakebite deaths.

HabitatFound near streams, in forests, and disturbed areas. Active day and night.
SymptomsImmediate pain, swelling spreading up limb, bleeding from gums, bruising, tissue death
Emergency ProtocolImmobilize victim completely. Keep limb at or below heart level. DO NOT use tourniquets. Evacuate IMMEDIATELY. Do not cut, suck, or ice wound.
CRITICAL
Reptile

Coral Snake (Micrurus)

Also known as: Coral Snake | Coral, Coralillo

Small, brightly banded snake (red, yellow/white, black bands). Neurotoxic venom causes respiratory paralysis. Symptoms may be delayed.

HabitatForests, leaf litter. Most active at night. Generally docile but will bite if handled.
SymptomsDelayed (hours): drooping eyelids, difficulty speaking, swallowing, breathing. May appear fine initially.
Emergency ProtocolImmobilize completely. Evacuate immediately. Requires specific antivenom. Watch breathing - may need rescue breathing.
CRITICAL
Reptile

Bushmaster (Lachesis muta)

Also known as: Bushmaster | Cascabela Muda, Toboba

Largest viper in Americas (2-3 meters). Reddish-brown with diamond pattern. Silent - no rattle. Found in deep forests.

HabitatDeep primary forest, near mammal burrows. Rare but extremely dangerous.
SymptomsSevere pain, rapid swelling, bleeding, shock. Fatality rate 80% untreated.
Emergency ProtocolSame as Fer-de-lance. Maximum urgency for evacuation. Most dangerous if multiple strikes.

Jumping Viper (Bothrops nummifer)

Also known as: Jumping Viper | Timbo, Saltadora

Short, thick-bodied viper to 1 meter. Can strike up to half body length upward. Brown with dark blotches.

HabitatForests, often near logs or rocks. Bites when stepped on or grabbed.
SymptomsSimilar to Fer-de-lance but generally less severe. Still medical emergency.
Emergency ProtocolImmobilize, keep calm, evacuate. Do not use tourniquet or cut wound.

Yellow-Jawed Tommigoff (Bothrops godmani)

Also known as: Yellow-Jawed Pit Viper | Tomigoff, Víbora de Fossa

Medium viper (1-1.5m). Green or brown with yellow jaw. Highly venomous hemotoxic bite.

HabitatCaribbean lowlands, forests, plantations. Very common in coastal regions.
SymptomsSevere pain, swelling, bleeding, tissue necrosis.
Emergency ProtocolImmobilize, evacuate immediately. Same protocol as Fer-de-lance.

Hognose Pit Viper (Porthidium nasutum)

Also known as: Hognose Pit Viper | Narigona, Víbora de Nariz

Small viper (60-80cm) with distinctive upturned snout. Brown with dark blotches.

HabitatForests, leaf litter, near streams. Often camouflaged.
SymptomsPain, swelling, localized tissue damage.
Emergency ProtocolImmobilize, seek medical attention. Antivenom may be needed.

Reptile

Central American Rattlesnake (Crotalus simus)

Also known as: Rattlesnake | Cascabel, Cantil

Large pit viper (1.5-2m) with distinctive rattle. Gray/brown with diamond pattern. Audible warning before striking.

HabitatDry forests, savannas, rocky areas. Pacific slope more common.
SymptomsSevere pain, swelling, bleeding, shock, respiratory failure.
Emergency ProtocolImmobilize, evacuate immediately. Do not approach even if rattle heard.

Green Iguana (Iguana iguana)

Also known as: Green Iguana | Iguana Verde, Garrobo

Large lizard (1.5-2m). Generally docile but will bite and scratch when threatened. Tail can cause painful whip-like strikes.

HabitatTrees near water, forests, gardens. Common throughout Honduras.
SymptomsBite wounds, scratches, possible infection.
Emergency ProtocolDo not handle without protection. If bitten, clean wound thoroughly and seek medical attention for infection risk.

Black Spiny-Tailed Iguana (Ctenosaura similis)

Also known as: Black Iguana | Iguana Negra, Garrobo Negro

Large lizard (1-1.5m). More aggressive than green iguana. Powerful bite and tail strikes.

HabitatDry forests, rocky areas, Pacific coast. Often seen sunning on rocks.
SymptomsSevere bite wounds, deep scratches, tail lacerations.
Emergency ProtocolAvoid handling. If bitten, clean wound, apply pressure if bleeding, seek medical care.

Giant Centipede (Scolopendra subspinipes)

Also known as: Giant Centipede | Ciempiés, Ciempies Gigante

Large centipede (15-20cm). Reddish-brown with many legs. Venomous bite causes severe pain.

HabitatUnder rocks, logs, leaf litter, inside shoes and clothing. Common in humid areas.
SymptomsImmediate severe pain, swelling, redness, possible nausea and fever.
Emergency ProtocolClean bite area, apply cold compress, take pain relievers. Seek medical help if severe reaction or child bitten.

Insect

Bullet Ant (Paraponera clavata)

Also known as: Bullet Ant | Hormiga Bala, Conga

Large black ant (2.5cm). Sting rated as most painful insect sting in world. Pain lasts 24+ hours.

HabitatRainforest trees, nests at base of trees. Caribbean lowlands.
SymptomsExcruciating pain (like gunshot), swelling, trembling, cold sweat.
Emergency ProtocolRemove stinger if present, clean area, apply cold compress, take pain medication. Pain will subside after 24 hours.
MODERATE

Tarantula (Brachypelma)

Also known as: Tarantula | Tarántula, Araña Peluda

Large hairy spider (10-15cm leg span). Black or brown. Bite painful but rarely dangerous. Urticating hairs cause skin irritation.

HabitatGround burrows, under logs, dry forests. Pacific slope common.
SymptomsPainful bite, skin rash from hairs, possible allergic reaction.
Emergency ProtocolClean bite, remove embedded hairs with tape, antihistamines for reaction. Seek help if breathing difficulty.

Brazilian Wandering Spider (Phoneutria)

Also known as: Wandering Spider | Araña de Plátano, Araña Errante

Large aggressive spider (5cm body). Dark brown, raises front legs when threatened. Highly venomous neurotoxin.

HabitatBanana plants, forests, sometimes enters homes. Rare but present.
SymptomsIntense pain, sweating, salivation, priapism (painful erection), respiratory difficulty.
Emergency ProtocolIMMEDIATE medical evacuation. Antivenom required. Keep victim calm and immobilized.

Scorpion (Centruroides)

Also known as: Scorpion | Alacrán, Escorpión

Small arachnid (5-10cm). Yellow to dark brown. Venomous sting, especially dangerous to children and elderly.

HabitatUnder rocks, bark, inside shoes, clothing, beds. Common in dry areas.
SymptomsImmediate burning pain, numbness, swelling, possible difficulty breathing (severe cases).
Emergency ProtocolClean sting site, apply cold compress, take pain relievers. Seek immediate medical help for children or severe reactions.

Insect

Kissing Bug (Triatoma)

Also known as: Kissing Bug, Assassin Bug | Chinche Besucona, Picuda

Medium bug (2-3cm). Brown/black with orange/red markings. Bites face, transmits Chagas disease. Long-term fatal if untreated.

HabitatThatch roofs, mud walls, cracks in housing. Rural areas especially.
SymptomsInitial: painless bite, swelling. Chronic (years): heart failure, digestive problems.
Emergency ProtocolAvoid sleeping in thatch/mud housing without protection. If bitten, clean area, seek medical testing for Chagas. Use bed nets.
CRITICAL
Insect

Mosquito (Aedes, Anopheles)

Also known as: Mosquito | Zancudo, Mosquito

Small flying insect. Transmits dengue, Zika, malaria, chikungunya. Leading cause of illness in Honduras.

HabitatStanding water, humid areas, dawn/dusk active. Everywhere.
SymptomsDengue: high fever, severe headache, joint pain. Malaria: cyclical fever, chills. Zika: mild fever, rash.
Emergency ProtocolUse DEET repellent, wear long sleeves, sleep under bed nets, eliminate standing water. Seek medical care for fever after bites.
HIGH
Insect

Botfly (Dermatobia hominis)

Also known as: Botfly, Human Botfly | Tórsalo, Mosca del Hombre

Large fly (1.5cm). Larvae burrow under skin causing painful boils. Larva breathes through small hole in skin.

HabitatForests, rural areas. Eggs carried by mosquitoes to humans.
SymptomsPainful lump with breathing hole, sensation of movement, possible infection.
Emergency ProtocolDo NOT squeeze. Cover breathing hole to suffocate larva, then extract surgically. Seek medical removal.
HIGH
Insect

Sand Flea (Tunga penetrans)

Also known as: Chigoe Flea, Jigger | Nigua, Pique

Tiny flea. Burrows into skin (especially toes, feet). Causes painful lesions, possible infection and tetanus.

HabitatSandy soil, beaches, dirt floors. Walk barefoot at your risk.
SymptomsSmall black dot in skin, painful swelling, pus, difficulty walking.
Emergency ProtocolDo not walk barefoot. If infested, sterilize needle, remove flea, clean wound, antibiotic ointment, tetanus shot.

Army Ants (Eciton)

Also known as: Army Ants, Driver Ants | Hormigas de Ejército, Hormigas Bravas

Columns of thousands of ants. Bite aggressively. Can overwhelm small animals. Nests move daily.

HabitatForests, moving in columns. Follow animal trails.
SymptomsMultiple painful bites, possible allergic reaction.
Emergency ProtocolMove away from column path. Do not camp in ant path. If bitten, move immediately, clean bites, monitor for reaction.

Cone-Nose Bug (Triatominae)

Also known as: Cone-Nose Bug | Chinche de Nariz Cónica

Related to kissing bug. Bites transmit parasites. Active at night.

HabitatRural housing, animal nests, woodpiles.
SymptomsBite marks, possible Chagas disease transmission.
Emergency ProtocolSeal cracks in housing, use bed nets, remove animal nests near dwelling.

Dieffenbachia (Dumbcane)

Also known as: Dumbcane, Mother-in-Law Tongue | Camotillo, Niqui

Large-leaved houseplant with green and white variegated leaves. Chewing causes immediate burning, throat swelling. Can cause fatal asphyxiation.

HabitatWidely cultivated, occasionally escaped into wild. Recognized by distinctive leaf pattern.
SymptomsImmediate burning mouth and throat, difficulty swallowing, swelling of airway.
Emergency ProtocolDO NOT INGEST. Wash hands thoroughly after handling. If ingested, rinse mouth, drink cold water, seek medical help if breathing compromised.

Flora

Manchineel Tree (Hippomane mancinella)

Also known as: Manchineel, Beach Apple | Manzanillo de la Muerte

Tree resembling apple, found on beaches. ALL PARTS TOXIC. Sap causes severe skin burns. Standing under tree during rain causes blisters.

HabitatCoastal beaches. Look for warning signs on tourist beaches. Avoid any tree with small green "apples" near coast.
SymptomsSkin blisters from sap or rain, severe eye damage (blindness if sap in eyes), burning throat if fruit eaten.
Emergency ProtocolAVOID COMPLETELY. If contact, rinse skin with large amounts of water immediately. If in eyes, flush 15+ minutes and seek medical help.
HIGH
Flora

Apocynaceae (Milky Sap Plants)

Also known as: Milkweed Family, Frangipani | Planta de Leche, Jazmín

Plants with milky white sap when cut or broken. Includes frangipani, oleander, and milkweeds. Sap causes severe irritation and systemic poisoning.

HabitatGardens, forests, disturbed areas. VERY common. If sap drips, do not touch.
SymptomsSkin irritation, blistering. Eye exposure causes blindness. Ingestion causes vomiting, heart irregularities, death.
Emergency ProtocolFlush eyes/skin IMMEDIATELY with copious water. Remove contaminated clothing. Seek medical help if ingested.
CRITICAL
Flora

Castor Bean (Ricinus communis)

Also known as: Castor Bean, Castor Oil Plant | Higuereta, Ricino, Palma Cristi

Shrub with large palmate leaves and spiky seed pods. Seeds contain ricin - one of most deadly poisons. 2-3 seeds can kill adult.

HabitatRoadsides, disturbed areas, cultivated. Common throughout Honduras.
SymptomsIngestion: severe vomiting, bloody diarrhea, organ failure. Contact: skin irritation.
Emergency ProtocolNEVER ingest any part. Do not burn (smoke toxic). Wear gloves when handling. If ingested, seek IMMEDIATE medical help.
CRITICAL
Flora

Oleander (Nerium oleander)

Also known as: Oleander | Adelfa, Laurel de Flor

Ornamental shrub with showy flowers (pink, white, red). ALL parts toxic. Smoke from burning is lethal.

HabitatGardens, roadsides, cultivated. Common in urban areas.
SymptomsNausea, vomiting, irregular heartbeat, confusion, death.
Emergency ProtocolDo not ingest, do not burn, do not use branches for cooking. Seek immediate medical help if ingested.

Datura (Jimson Weed)

Also known as: Jimson Weed, Devil's Trumpet | Toloache, Estramonio, Floripondio

Plant with large trumpet flowers and spiky seed pods. All parts contain dangerous tropane alkaloids.

HabitatDisturbed areas, roadsides, abandoned fields. Common.
SymptomsDilated pupils, dry mouth, hallucinations, rapid heartbeat, confusion, seizures, death.
Emergency ProtocolDo not ingest any part. Traditional use is extremely dangerous. Seek immediate medical help if ingested.

Physic Nut (Jatropha curcas)

Also known as: Physic Nut, Purge Nut | Piñón, Tempate, Jatropa

Shrub with lobed leaves and clusters of round seeds. Seeds are purgative and toxic in quantity.

HabitatHedges, fences, cultivated areas. Very common.
SymptomsNausea, severe diarrhea, vomiting, dehydration.
Emergency ProtocolDo not eat seeds. Leaves sometimes used medicinally but only by experts. Avoid internal use.

Sandbox Tree (Hura crepitans)

Also known as: Sandbox Tree, Dynamite Tree | Árbol de Caja, Habillo

Large tree with explosive seed pods that shoot seeds at 150 mph. Sap is toxic and causes skin irritation.

HabitatLowland forests, Caribbean slope. Distinctive pumpkin-shaped fruit.
SymptomsSkin irritation from sap, injury from explosive seeds.
Emergency ProtocolDo not stand near ripe fruit pods. Wear eye protection near tree. Wash skin if sap contact.

Spurge Family (Euphorbia)

Also known as: Spurge, Poinsettia Family | Euforbia, Lechetreza

Plants with milky sap. Includes many ornamental and wild species. Sap causes severe skin and eye irritation.

HabitatEverywhere - gardens, wild, disturbed areas. Extremely common.
SymptomsSkin blistering, eye damage, blindness if sap in eyes, vomiting if ingested.
Emergency ProtocolDo not touch sap. If contact, wash immediately. If in eyes, flush 15+ minutes and seek medical help.

Cockspur (Coral Bean)

Also known as: Coral Bean, Cockspur | Colorín, Bucares

Tree with bright red seeds in pods. Seeds contain toxins similar to ricin. Beautiful but deadly.

HabitatForests, cultivated as ornamental. Distinctive red and black seeds.
SymptomsIngestion: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, seizures, death.
Emergency ProtocolNever ingest seeds. Do not make jewelry from seeds (handling risk). Keep children away.
